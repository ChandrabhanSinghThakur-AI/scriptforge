# Screenplay Format Guide

## Scene Headings (Slug Lines)

Always written in CAPS. Format: INT./EXT. LOCATION - TIME OF DAY

Examples:
INT. CORPORATE OFFICE - DAY
EXT. MUMBAI STREET - NIGHT
INT./EXT. CAR (MOVING) - CONTINUOUS

## Action/Description

Written in present tense. Brief, visual. What the camera sees.
- No "we see" or "camera moves"
- Short paragraphs (3-4 lines max)
- Sound effects in CAPS: A GUNSHOT echoes.

Example:
Rahul walks in. Expensive shoes click on marble. He surveys the open-plan office — twenty developers hunched over laptops, energy drinks sweating on desks.

## Character Introduction

First time a character appears: NAME in CAPS, followed by brief description with age.

Example:
RAHUL SINGH (34), sharp jaw, sharper suit. The kind of man who reads the room before the room reads him.

## Dialogue

Character name CENTERED in CAPS.
Dialogue below, not in caps.
Parentheticals in (brackets) for tone/action.

RAHUL
(surveying the chaos)
Yeh startup hai ya refugee camp?

INTERN
(nervous)
Sir, actually hum log crunch mein—

RAHUL
Crunch. Interesting word. Main tumhe ek better word deta hoon — accountability.

## Parentheticals

Use sparingly. Only when tone isn't obvious from dialogue.
(beat) — pause
(sotto) — under breath
(O.S.) — off screen
(V.O.) — voice over
(CONT'D) — character continues after action

## Transitions

RIGHT-ALIGNED in caps:
CUT TO:
SMASH CUT:
DISSOLVE TO:
MATCH CUT:

Use sparingly. Modern scripts rarely use them.

## Montage

MONTAGE - RAHUL'S FIRST WEEK

- Rahul reviews financial statements, finds discrepancies
- Rahul fires the accounts team one by one
- Rahul presents clean books to the board, gets a standing ovation

END MONTAGE

## Page Timing

1 page = approximately 1 minute of screen time.
- Feature film: 90-120 pages
- TV episode (1 hr): 45-60 pages
- Web series episode: 20-35 pages

## Hinglish Dialogue Tips (Indian Web Series)

- Mix Hindi and English naturally as Indians actually speak
- Corporate jargon stays in English: "deliverables", "burn rate", "runway"
- Emotions in Hindi: "Teri himmat kaise hui"
- Code-switching for emphasis: "I don't give a damn. Samjhe?"
- Slang establishes character class/region
- Avoid transliteration inconsistency — pick one romanization style

## Scene Structure (Mini-Arc)

Every scene needs:
1. GOAL — what does the character want?
2. CONFLICT — what stops them?
3. OUTCOME — did they get it? (usually no, or with a cost)

If a scene has no conflict, cut it.

## Show Don't Tell

BAD:
RAHUL
Main bahut ambitious hoon.

GOOD:
Rahul glances at the CEO's corner office. His jaw tightens. He straightens his tie, takes a breath, and walks the other direction — toward the finance wing.

## Subtext

Characters rarely say what they mean.

On the surface: "Nice presentation, bro."
Subtext: "I'll destroy you in the next meeting."

Write dialogue where meaning lives BETWEEN the lines.
