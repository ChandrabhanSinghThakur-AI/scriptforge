# Story Structure — The Three-Act Framework

## Act 1: Setup (First 25%)

### Opening Image
The very first shot/scene. Shows the "before" world. Contrast with final image.

### Theme Stated
Someone (not the protagonist) says the theme out loud — the lesson the hero needs to learn.
Usually happens in first 5-10 minutes.

### Set-Up
Introduce protagonist's world, flaws, relationships. 
Show what's WRONG with their life (even if they don't know it yet).

### Catalyst (Inciting Incident)
Something happens that changes everything. No going back.
- Gets fired
- Receives a letter
- Witnesses something
- Someone arrives/leaves

### Debate
Hero resists the call. "Should I do this?" Internal conflict.

### Break into Act 2
Hero makes a CHOICE. Enters the new world. Crosses the threshold.

---

## Act 2: Confrontation (Middle 50%)

### B-Story
Secondary story — usually relationship/love interest.
Carries the THEME. Provides emotional relief from A-story intensity.

### Fun and Games
The "promise of the premise." 
Why the audience came. The trailer moments.
Hero explores new world, has early wins.

### Midpoint
FALSE VICTORY or FALSE DEFEAT.
- Stakes get raised
- Time clock starts
- Hero commits fully (no more "just visiting")

### Bad Guys Close In
Everything tightens. External: villains regroup. Internal: team fractures.
Jealousy, suspicion, paranoia.

### All Is Lost
The lowest point. Something DIES (literal or metaphorical).
- Mentor dies
- Relationship breaks
- Gets caught/exposed
- Business collapses

### Dark Night of the Soul
Hero processes the loss. Grief. Reflection.
The THEME clicks. Hero realizes what they need to change.

### Break into Act 3
Hero finds the solution. A new plan. Combines A-story and B-story lessons.

---

## Act 3: Resolution (Final 25%)

### Finale
Hero faces the final challenge using everything they've learned.
- Gathers the team
- Executes the plan
- Confronts the antagonist
- Uses the theme

### Final Image
Mirror of opening image. Shows how the world has CHANGED.
Same location, different person.

## Web Series Adaptation

For episodic web series, each episode is a mini three-act structure:

Episode structure (25 min):
- Cold open / hook (2 min) — in media res, grab attention
- Act 1 setup (5 min) — episode's specific problem
- Act 2 escalation (12 min) — complications, B-story
- Act 3 climax (4 min) — resolution or cliffhanger
- Tag/stinger (2 min) — setup for next episode

Season arc overlays on top:
- Episodes 1-2: Season setup, introduce world
- Episodes 3-6: Rising complications
- Episode 7-8: Midpoint reversal
- Episodes 9-10: All is lost + climax

## Character Arc Types

### Positive Arc
Hero starts flawed → learns theme → becomes better
(Most common in commercial storytelling)

### Negative Arc (Fall)
Hero starts okay → rejects theme → becomes worse/destroyed
(Breaking Bad, Macbeth, corporate villain origin stories)

### Flat Arc
Hero already knows the truth → changes the WORLD around them
(Sherlock, James Bond)

### Disillusionment Arc
Hero believes a lie → discovers harsh truth → accepts dark reality
(Noir, cynical thrillers)

---

## Conflict Types

Every scene needs at least one:

1. Character vs Character — direct opposition
2. Character vs Self — internal struggle
3. Character vs System — fighting institutions
4. Character vs Time — deadline pressure
5. Character vs Truth — avoiding/accepting reality

For corporate drama, #3 and #5 are gold.

---

## Dialogue Rules

1. Every character must sound DIFFERENT (speech pattern, vocabulary, rhythm)
2. Dialogue is NOT conversation — it's compressed, has purpose
3. Cut the first and last line of every dialogue exchange (usually filler)
4. Use silence. What characters DON'T say matters more.
5. Arguments are more interesting than agreements
6. People talk AT each other, not TO each other (competing agendas)
